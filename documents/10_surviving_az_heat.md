# Surviving the Arizona Heat on Campus

Phoenix-area summers are brutal, and the Tempe campus bakes. This is the
survival knowledge nobody puts in the course catalog.

## Hydrate before you're thirsty
Carry a refillable water bottle everywhere. ASU has water fountains and bottle
fillers across campus for refills. Once you feel thirsty it's already too late —
sip water steadily throughout the day rather than chugging when you're parched.

## Find shade and use campus cooling
Shade is everything outdoors. The Memorial Union (MU) is a popular gathering
spot with shade sails, awnings, and building overhangs; the PowerParasol
photovoltaic shade structure in front of the MU keeps that space usable even in
peak summer. ASU runs miles of chilled-water piping and mechanical cooling
across hundreds of buildings, so duck indoors between classes. The light rail
and FLASH shuttles are also air-conditioned ways to cross campus without baking.

## Dress and protect yourself
Wear lightweight, light-colored clothing and bring a hat, sunglasses, and even
an umbrella for portable shade. Sunscreen matters even on short walks between
buildings.

## Time your outdoor activity
Avoid being outside during the hottest part of the day (roughly midday to late
afternoon). Schedule walks, workouts, and errands for early morning or evening
when possible.

## Watch for heat illness
Know the warning signs of heat exhaustion and heat stroke — dizziness, nausea,
headache, confusion, or stopping sweating. Look out for friends, and get to
air conditioning and water immediately if symptoms appear.
